> **Home robot:** 📋 Patchbay (PM). Files the result into `TASKS.md`; strategy calls escalate to Otto.

## When to use
More work than time. A backlog, a roadmap, a week. The user is asking *what first*, and often really asking
*what can I drop*.

## Steps

1. **Get the list out of their head.** Everything, unranked, unfiltered. Do not let them pre-edit; the item
   they're embarrassed to say is often the one that matters.

2. **Find the goal it all serves.** Priority is meaningless without it. If there are two competing goals, say
   so — that is the real problem, and it belongs to **Otto**, not to a scoring rubric.

3. **Score each item, visibly:**
   - **Impact** — on the *stated goal*, not on general goodness. 1–5.
   - **Effort** — honest, in hours or days. 1–5. Ask if you don't know; do not guess for them.
   - **Confidence** — how sure are we the impact is real? 1–5. This is the column people skip and shouldn't.

   Rank by `(Impact × Confidence) ÷ Effort`. Show the arithmetic. A ranking nobody can argue with is a
   ranking nobody trusts.

4. **Name what falls below the line.** Explicitly. A priority list without a *not-doing* list is a wish list.
   Say what is being deferred and what it costs to defer it.

5. **Sanity-check the top of the list.** Is the highest-ranked item something they could start today? If the
   top item is blocked, the real top item is whatever unblocks it.

6. **File it.** Write the ranked list into `TASKS.md` as an atomic checklist, each item small enough to
   finish and verify in one pass.

## Guardrails
- Scores are a conversation, not a verdict. If the number disagrees with the user's gut, interrogate both.
- Never rank on effort alone — that's how a company spends a year on easy things.
- Nothing gets started here. Ranking is not doing; hand the top item to the robot who owns it.
- Keep it terse. A prioritisation that takes an hour to read has failed.
