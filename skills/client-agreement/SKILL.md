## When to use
The user is a solopreneur about to send a client agreement or SOW, or a client sent one first and the user
wants it reviewed before they sign. Either direction, the shape of the risk is the same: one side has a legal
department and a template they've used a hundred times, and the other side has neither.

**Say once, plainly, at the top of the output: this is a starting draft, not legal advice. Have a qualified
lawyer in your jurisdiction review anything before it is signed.** Once — not sprayed over every clause.

## Steps

1. **Confirm which side the user is on.** Drafting from scratch, or reviewing something a client sent? A
   clause that protects the drafter is a trap for whoever signs without reading it. Ask if it isn't obvious
   from context.

2. **Cover the clauses that decide whether the solopreneur gets paid and gets to stop**, in this order:
   - **Scope and change orders.** Define what's *in*, and say plainly that anything not listed is out. "Reasonable
     revisions" with no number attached means unlimited — write the number.
   - **Payment terms.** Net-30 is normal. Net-60 is a cashflow weapon aimed at someone with no AR department —
     say so in those words. A deposit before work starts is the single strongest protection available; flag its
     absence.
   - **Kill fee.** If the client cancels mid-project, does the solopreneur get paid for work already done? No
     kill fee means they eat the loss on someone else's decision.
   - **IP ownership and transfer.** Who owns the work, and *when* — on final payment, or on delivery? "On
     delivery" means the client can walk away unpaid and keep the work. This is the trap freelancers miss most.
   - **Liability cap.** Uncapped liability on a solopreneur means one bad outcome can take their house. A cap
     tied to fees paid is standard; its absence is not a formality, it's the whole risk of the contract.
   - **Termination.** Can either side leave, with what notice, and is work-in-progress paid on exit? A contract
     the client can end at will with no kill fee is a contract with no floor under it.

3. **Rank by what it costs if it goes wrong**, not by document order. Lead with the clause that would actually
   hurt — usually payment terms, IP transfer, or liability, in some order, not the definitions section.

4. **Answer the question they didn't ask.** Someone asking about payment terms usually also needs to know
   whether they own their own work until they're paid for it. Say so unprompted.

5. **Flag what needs a real lawyer** — name the specific clause, not the whole document. A liability cap that
   seems low, an indemnity that seems broad, anything touching a licence or a regulated industry.

6. **If enforceability turns on a jurisdiction's actual rule** — a non-compete's scope, a required notice
   period, a licence requirement — do not guess. Say "unverified" and hand it to Sonar.

## Guardrails
- **Rate and pricing structure are Baudrate's**, not this skill's. This skill checks whether the *terms*
  protect the person doing the work; it doesn't say what the work is worth.
- Never invent a statute, a standard contract term, or a jurisdiction's default rule. "Unverified" is a
  complete answer.
- Plain English over legalese wherever the law permits. Translate a term of art once, then use it.
- Print the **full absolute filepath** of any draft agreement written for the human to read.
