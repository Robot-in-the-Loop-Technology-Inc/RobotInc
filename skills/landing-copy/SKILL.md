> Bitforge, evidence from Sonar. Any claim that could read as a warranty goes past Docket.

## When to use
The user needs words that sell: a landing page, a launch announcement, a value proposition, an ad.

## Steps

1. **Find out what it actually does.** Read the code, the README, the product. **Never write a claim you have
   not verified** — an unearned promise is a support ticket and, sometimes, a legal problem.

2. **Name the pain before the product.** Who is this for, what is broken in their day, and why now? If you
   cannot name the person, ask. "Teams" is not a person.

3. **Write the page:**
   - **Hero** — one headline stating the *outcome*, not the feature. One subhead saying who it's for.
   - **Three benefit blocks** — each an outcome, each with the mechanism underneath in one line.
   - **Social proof scaffold** — the shape of the proof, honestly marked `[placeholder]` until it's real.
   - **One CTA.** One. Repeated, not multiplied.

4. **Pricing framing** — name and frame the tiers around jobs-to-be-done, anchoring value rather than listing
   features. The actual tier structure and the numbers are **Baudrate's**; get them, don't invent them.

5. **SEO** — title tag, meta description, and the primary keyword matched to real search intent (Sonar can
   source what people actually search).

6. **Cut it.** Delete every sentence that would survive being replaced with the word "obviously".

## Guardrails
- Concrete over clever. Benefit-led, never buzzword soup. No "revolutionary", no "seamless", no "unlock".
- **Never state a capability the product does not have**, or a metric nobody measured. A superlative is a claim.
- Comparative and guarantee-shaped claims ("the fastest", "never lose data") go past **Docket** — they can be
  read as warranties.
- Match the user's own voice if they have one. Read what they've already published.
- Print the **full absolute filepath** of the copy you write so the human can review it.
